MISTERI

kuyang

Another Indonesian Horror Story comes from the island of Kalimantan called Kuyang. His figure is like a human during the day and blends in with society, but at night he transforms into a scary ghost. He was able to remove the head from the body. Not only that, the head can fly along with internal organs such as the heart, liver and intestines. When flying, a red light resembling fire appeared following him from behind. It has two fangs on the right and left of its mouth. Kuyang is believed to be a ghost who likes to drink the blood of mothers who are pregnant or have just given birth, as well as the blood of newborn babies. So that his victims do not become suspicious, Kuyang is usually able to transform himself into a cat or a bird.

MISTERI

Pocong

Pocong is a type of ghost in the form of a human wrapped in a shroud. In Indonesia, this kind of ghost is also known as a wrap ghost. Pocong is also known as the shroud ghost. Pocong is a ghost who is said to be the spirit of a dead person trapped in their shroud.

MISTERI

Kuntilanak

Kuntilanak (Melayu: pontianak or puntianak) or often abbreviated as kunti is a ghost that is believed to come from a pregnant woman who died or a woman who died in childbirth and the child had not yet been born (miscarriage). The name "puntianak" is an abbreviation of "woman who died giving birth".[1] This myth is similar to the myth of the Langsuir ghost which is known in Southeast Asia, especially in the archipelago such as Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei Darussalam, southern Philippines (Mindanao) and Thailand (Pattani).[2] The myth of the kuntilanak ghost has long been a myth in the archipelago.

MISTERI

Genderuwo

Genderuwo (in Javanese pronunciation: Gěnděruwå) is a Javanese myth about a kind of jinn or spirits in the form of humans resembling apes who are large and stocky with reddish black skin, their bodies are covered with thick hair that grows all over their bodies. Genderuwo is known most widely in society on the island of Java, Indonesia. Sundanese people call it "gandaruwa" and Javanese people generally call it "gendruwo".
